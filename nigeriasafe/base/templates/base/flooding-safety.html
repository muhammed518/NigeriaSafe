Flooding Safety

Stay safe when floodwaters rise.

Before a Flood

Understanding the "why" and "where" of your risk can save you thousands in damages.

  • Determine Your Flood Zone: Check Flood Map Service Center (or your local equivalent). Even if you aren't in a "high-risk" zone, 25% of all flood claims come from moderate-to-low-risk areas.
  • The "Go Bag" Essentials:

    • Documents: Store copies of birth certificates, insurance policies, and IDs in a waterproof "dry bag." Power: A hand-crank radio and portable power banks. Water: At least one gallon per person per day for at least three days.
    food: Non-perishable items like energy bars, canned goods, and dried fruits.
    First Aid: A basic kit with bandages, antiseptics, and any personal medications.

    Property Mitigation:
    Sumps & Valves: Install a sump pump with a battery backup. Install backwater valves on your sewer lines to prevent sewage from backing up into your drains (a common and messy flood byproduct).
    Utilities: If you have time, turn off electricity at the main breaker and gas at the main valve to prevent fires and explosions.

During a Flood

Many people underestimate the force exerted by moving water. It isn't just "wet"—it is heavy and relentless.

  • Hidden Hazards: * Electrocution: If water reaches your electrical outlets, the water itself can become energized.
  • Displacement: Floods often displace wildlife. Be aware of snakes, rodents, or insects seeking higher ground—potentially in your home.
  • Vertical Evacuation: If you are trapped in a building, move to the highest floor. Only go to the roof if necessary. Do not climb into a closed attic, as you may become trapped by rising water.

After a Flood

The danger doesn't evaporate when the water does.

  • Structural Integrity: Floodwaters can undermine foundations and cause "scour" around support beams. If you see new cracks in drywall or doors that won't close, the foundation may have shifted.
  • The 24-48 Hour Window: This is the critical timeframe before mold begins to grow.

Everything porous (carpet, padding, drywall, insulation) that touched floodwater usually needs to be discarded.
Floodwater is often "Category 3" (Black Water), meaning it contains sewage, chemicals, and heavy metals.

Extreme Heat Safety

How to stay safe during periods of extreme heat.

Preparing for Extreme Heat

Preparing your home is about managing radiant heat (sunlight) and convection (air movement).

  • Reflective Shielding: Window reflectors (like the silver foil ones for cars) reflect up to 80% of incoming solar radiation. Light-colored curtains are better than dark ones, as dark fabrics absorb heat and radiate it back into the room.
  • The Fan Fallacy: Fans do not cool the air; they cool you by accelerating the evaporation of sweat. However, once the ambient temperature exceeds 35°C (95°F), fans can actually speed up dehydration by blowing air that is hotter than your body temperature across your skin.
  • Insulation: Weatherstrip doors and windows to keep the cool air in and the "heat soak" out.

Staying Safe During Extreme Heat

Your body’s primary cooling mechanism is evaporative cooling. If you stop sweating, you are in immediate danger.

  • Strategic Hydration: Don't wait for thirst—that’s a late-stage signal. Avoid caffeine and alcohol, which act as diuretics and can worsen dehydration.
  • The "Vascular Cooling" Trick: To cool down fast, run cool water over your "pulse points"—wrists, neck, and temples. These areas have blood vessels close to the surface, helping to lower your core temperature more efficiently.
  • Vehicle Safety: Even on a mild 21°C (70°F) day, the inside of a car can reach 40°C (104°F) in just 20 minutes. Cracking the windows has a negligible effect on temperature reduction.

Recognizing Heat-Related Illnesses

It is vital to distinguish between Exhaustion (your body is struggling) and Stroke (your body’s cooling system has failed).
Symptom Comparison
Heat Exhaustion | Heat Stroke (Emergency!)
Skin Moist, pale, and clammy | Dry, hot, and red Sweating
Heavy sweating | No sweating (usually)
Mental State Normal or slightly faint | Confused, combative, or unconscious
Pulse Fast and weak | Fast and strong
Body Temp Below 39°C (103°F) | Above 39°C (103°F)
